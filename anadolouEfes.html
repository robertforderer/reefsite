Executive Summary

Anadolu Efes Brewery and Malt Industry is the largest company in producing and distributing malt, beer, and plastic crates in Turkey. It holds about 78% of the Turkish beer market share. The current goals of Anadolu Efes is to improve their distribution network to reduce transportation costs, and also a capacity expansion plan. With such a large share in the beer market, it is anticipated that demand will increase over the years. This following report and analysis uses a method called linear programming to determine the most cost effective amount of breweries to open, and when to expand to ensure so that they can fulfill the anticipated increase in demand. Our team found that Izmir was the best location to build a brewery because of savings in long term shipping costs.

Introduction

Anadolu Efes is a Turkish beer company that processes malt, brews beer, and finally distributes this beer to consumers. Anadolu Efes Brewery and Malt Industry Corporation currently owns two breweries and is looking to expand. Their current breweries are already fully expanded and close to their capacity limits. The company already sent technical staff to scout possible new locations for the breweries. Based on the water quality, beer demand concentration, and the distance between malt plants and breweries, the technical staff chose the following: Izmir, Sakarya, and Adana. Team Alfa will be using a mathematical process called linear programming to determine the most cost effective amount of breweries to open, when to open and expand them, and the cheapest transportation and production plan for Anadolu Efes. The process of linear programming utilizes multiple data inputs and subjects them to limitations or constraints in order to make particular business decisions. These decisions are made with the goal of either minimizing or maximizing an equation called “the objective function”. In the case of Anadolu Efes, the objective function minimizes the construction, expansion and transportations costs.

Model and Methodology

Anadolu Efes is planning to add up to 3 more breweries to its two step transportation process with three separate location categories. The first category is malt plant sites. Anadolu Efes has three malt plant sites: Afyon, Konya, and Izmir. The malt plants in Afyon and Konya receive domestic malt while the malt plant in Izmir receives imported malt. The second location category is breweries. Anadolu Efes currently operates two breweries, one in Istanbul and one in Ankara. They are currently considering three possible new brewery locations: Izmir, Sakarya, and Adana. The third location category is distribution centers. Anadolu Efes has six distribution centers: Istanbul, Izmir, Antalya, Bursa, Kayseri, as well as an export center in Izmir. Team Alfa built a model resembling this distribution system with the goal of finding the lowest cost. To find the lowest cost, Team Alfa has to consider shipping cost and construction cost. One of the biggest costs would be constructing a brewery and expanding a brewery. When considering brewery construction and expansion decisions, we must consider building and transportation costs together. We built the model to consider the next 20 years of operation. Our model is constrained by the following factors: a brewery must be built before it is expanded, building and expanding can only be done once per brewery, neither the malt plants’ nor brewery’s capacities can be exceeded, the demand of each distribution centers must be met, and malt transported to each brewery must be processed into beer and sent to distribution centers. (See appendix for Model formulation)

Model Assumptions

Anadolu Efes is a large company that has many operating costs including production, marketing, and corporate costs. Our model does not take all of these costs into account. Another key limitation is the fact that our model only accounts for one ingredient, malt, in the production of beer. Beer also requires sugar, hops, and water. Our transportation model does not account for the transportation costs of these ingredients. Furthermore, we assume that there is no spilling during the transportation process. If there is any spilling, more malt, more beer, and more transportation expenditures will be required to fulfill the demand of the various distribution centers.

One of the key assumptions of our model is that the demand forecast is accurate. Our model accounts for the next twenty years of operation. Forecasting demand far into the future is extremely difficult; therefore, our team assumes that the demand in year three stays the same in years four through twenty. In the first year the breweries in Istanbul and Ankara have 18 million liters of excess brewing capacity to meet any fluctuations in demand. In the second year after building a brewery the model expresses an excess 49 million liters of brewing capacity. Finally, in the third through twentieth year, the model expresses an excess of 40 million liters of brewing capacity. The forecasts for the first, second and third years are probably fairly accurate and Team Alfa is comfortable with the excess capacity cushions stated above. However, assuming that demand will stay the same in years three through twenty is a dangerous assumption. The probability that demand will increase by more than 40 million liters in the next twenty years is very high. Therefore, more effort should be allocated to forecasting for the next twenty years. Furthermore, Anadolu Efes should be aware that the distribution model suggested by Team Alfa will need to change if total beer demand reaches a level of 540 million liters. Nevertheless, any fluctuation in beer demand would only be reflected in phase two, the brewing process. Even if there is a large fluctuation in demand, Konya has enough excess malt processing capacity to supply more than 400 million liters of beer. On the other hand, if demand suddenly decreases, additional options for downsizing the distribution center might be considered. Despite the fact that our model considers twenty years, decision makers must be aware that operational decisions must be made every few years in order to adapt to the current business environment.

Analysis and Findings

Team Alfa found that building a brewery in Izmir in year 2 and expanding that brewery in year 3 is the cheapest solution to satisfying Anadolu Efes’ growing demand. A brewery must be built by year 2 and expanded by year 3 to satisfy the company’s growing demand. Nevertheless, the construction of the brewery should be delayed to year 2 because the total cost of the distribution and production plan would increase by approximately 3 million dollars if the brewery was built in year 1. In order for a brewery construction in Izmir to take place in year 1, the price of the brewery would have to drop from 75 million dollars to 32 million dollars. Furthermore, the interest rate is an important factor when considering when to build a new brewery. Team Alfa found that constructing a new brewery would be optimal if interest rates dropped from 10% to 4%.

With the new addition of the Izmir brewery, relationships between the malt plants, breweries, and demand nodes change slightly throughout the plan. In year 1, no malt is imported through the port of Izmir. Once a brewery is built in year 2, approximately 8 thousand tons of malt is imported. Once the brewery is expanded in year 3, approximately 13 thousand tons of malt is imported. Imported malt is a cheaper option than domestic malt because shipping malt between the port, brewery, and distribution center in Izmir is extremely cost effective.

Other than importing malt, malt plant output and the relationships between malt plants and breweries will remain similar throughout the model. The Afyon malt plant will deliver most of its output to Istanbul and a small amount of its output to Ankara. Konya will continue to deliver its entire output to Ankara. (See appendix for exact shipping and output figures)

Some of the relationships between breweries and distribution centers will change as well. The Istanbul demand node will continue to receive all of its beer from the Istanbul brewery. The distribution center in Izmir used to satisfy some demand from the Istanbul brewery and some of its demand from the Ankara brewery. Once the brewery is opened in Izmir, most of the Izmir demand will be satisfied with Izmir brewery while some will be satisfied with the Ankara brewery. Once the brewery is expanded, all of Izmir’s demand will be satisfied by output from the Izmir brewery. Team Alfa decided in part 1 of our analysis that Antalya should receive its beer from Ankara instead of Istanbul. Antalya’s will continue to receive beer from the brewery in Ankara throughout our model. However after Izmir’s expansion, a small amount of beer will be shipped from Izmir to Antalya. Team Alfa in part 1 of our analysis decided that Bursa should receive beer from Istanbul instead of Ankara. Throughout our model, Bursa continues to receive all of its beer from Istanbul. The entirety of Kayseri’s demand is be satisfied by Ankara in the original plan and every year of Team Alfa’s model. And lastly, beer will be exported from Izmir as soon as a brewery is opened in Izmir. (See exact shipping and production figures in the appendix)

Conclusion: High-level discussion of findings and recommendations. This section should be geared towards a nontechnical executive.

The analysis above addresses the most cost effective capacity expansion plan for Anadolu Efes to satisfy the anticipated increase in demand, and also maintain its high share in the beer market. Team Alfa’s recommendations to Anadolu Efes is to build a new brewery in Izmir in year two, then expanding this brewery in year 3. Building in year 2 as opposed to year 1 saves Anadolu Efes three million dollars. Although Izmir is the most costly to build and expand, the long term savings associated with its location make Izmir the best place to build a brewery. The main relationships that change between our model and the existing model are changing the brewery-distribution pairs from Ankara/Bursa and Istanbul/Antalya to Ankara/Antalya and Istanbul/Bursa. Throughout the capacity expansion in the next three years, most of the relationships between Malt plants, Breweries, and Distribution Centers remain the same. However, Izmir’s brewery addition leads to Izmir distribution center receiving beer from Izmir. This new brewery also leads to the importing of malt as part of the distribution plan. Our distribution plan required looking forward twenty years in order to make the brewery construction decision; however, future changes in the external environment may lead to changes in the distribution plan.
