NFL Scheduling: Optimization of flying distance

Executive Summary

Our team has developed a model for deciding the NFL (National Football League) game schedule for one season. We used linear programming in order to formulate a solution that will reduce the cumulative miles traveled by all NFL teams. To formulate the problem, we used data for the distance between each team and every possible opponent in the NFL. We then implemented the constraints and assumptions that affect the current NFL schedule. We were then able to develop a linear program using Open Solver to arrive at an optimal distance. The optimization of the NFL schedule will result in lower traveling costs. In addition to traveling costs, our solution has implications such as reducing fuels costs and environmental impact of energy use to travel.

Introduction

Every year the NFL is confronted with a specific scheduling problem. Which teams are going to play each other and when? In addition, travel plans must be made in order to get teams from their hometown to the opponent's stadium. One factor that the NFL does not seem to consider in their formulation of this problem is the effects that all of the travel and fuel consumption will have on the environment. In addition, the distance traveled can also affect team’s overall strength and performance due to the time spent in the air. In the 2016 season, NFL teams will travel a cumulative distance totaling more than 500,000 miles (cbssports.com). Jet fuel costs approximately 5 dollars per gallon (aviationweek) and a Boeing 747 is estimated to use 5 gallons per mile (Howstuffworks.com). Therefore, the NFL’s immense traveling schedule incurs large economic and environmental costs. Our group will use a method called linear programming to create a more cost effective and environmentally friendly schedule.

In addition to creating an NFL schedule that minimized the distance traveled. Our group also heuristically assigned home and away games in order to close the gap in traveling inequality. The current top 3 teams who travel the most are Los Angeles, Oakland, Seattle. Rams accumulated 37,000 miles during 2016 partly because Rams moved from St. Louis to Los Angeles. The Rams, Seahawks, and Raiders accumulated a total of 85,000 miles. West coast teams typically travel more miles than east coast teams because most teams are located on the east coast. Our group attempted to assign home and away games in order to create more equality in terms of traveling distance among the teams. However, heuristically assigning home and away games proved to be more difficult than we thought, so we were unable to perform this task.

Problem Description

Linear programming is a system of formulation that contains one objective determined by various decisions. These decisions are also subject to constraints. The objective of our model is to create an NFL schedule that minimizes the total distance traveled by all 32 teams over the course of a single 17-week season. We must decide on every possible matchup in every possible week. The constraints in our model are the scheduling customs of the NFL.

In order to create a fair and competitive schedule, certain rules must be applied to our plan. First, each team can only play one game per week in the 17 week NFL schedule. Football is a physically demanding sport. Therefore, if teams competed more than once a week, more players would acquire injuries. Another measure used to reduce player injuries is that every team gets one week off, called a “bye”. This means every team plays a total of 16 games with one bye. Four teams will receive byes in weeks 5 through 12.

Another set of rules that dictate the NFL schedule are related to the NFL conference and division system. Figure 1 below displays the breakdown of the NFL’s 32 teams and how they are divided into two 16 team conferences: the NFC and the AFC. These two conferences are then divided into four divisions containing four teams each. Every team plays every other team in its own division twice, totally six inter-division games for each team. In addition, each team also plays teams out of its division but in its conference six times. Finally, each team plays four teams in the opposite conference. The sum of these three categories is sixteen. A complete, seventeen week NFL schedule is created once one bye per team is added.

Figure 1: NFL Conference & Division Breakdown

The last important rule in the NFL scheduling problem is the fact that non-division teams cannot play each other more than once. The number of teams, and the number of games allows teams to not play each other. Every season, each team plays only 13 out of 32 teams. This fact leads to many possible variations of the NFL schedule. Therefore, our objective is to use linear programming to locate the variation that minimizes the total distance traveled.

A key assumption of our problem is that each NFL team travels the same. Therefore, one mile traveled by one team will be counted the same as one mile traveled by another. Furthermore, we are only considering air travel miles between each city, and excluding other travel distances such as buses to the stadiums and hotel. Another assumption is that each team will fly back home after each away game. In reality, teams typically fly home to regroup even if they have an away game the next week. In addition, we will not be considering the schedule of other games or events occurring in each stadium that might cause a scheduling conflict. Our last assumption is that we are only accounting for domestic games. We will not be considering games played in London because there are only three games played in London out of a total of 256 regular season games.

Results

Our problem contained more than 17,000 variables by considering every possible game in every possible week. Nevertheless, robust Open Solver software was able to solve the problem in approximately ten minutes. The schedule resembled a normal NFL schedule, with every team playing sixteen games with a bye. Furthermore, the scheduling traditions of the NFL described above were also met. The software produced a schedule totaling slightly more than 380,000 miles. This schedule reduces the number of miles traveled by teams this season by approximately 28 percent. Even if three games were played in London and one game in Mexico City, our model would still have a shorter distance than the current schedule.

Conclusion

While the NFL's focus is not scheduling with travel distance in mind, it was the sole focus of our group's report. Teams in our model travel slightly more than 380,000 miles while NFL teams this year will travel more than 500,000 miles. The positive benefits of a schedule with less traveling include increased player performance, money savings, and lessened environmental impact. Nevertheless, the schedule we produced could not be used by the NFL right now. The current NFL schedule is based on the fact that all thirty-two teams could not possibly play one another in a sixteen game schedule. Therefore, the actual NFL schedulers have a multiple-year perspective in their approach. They make sure that every team plays one another every few years. If our current one-year schedule was used every year, certain teams would never play one another. Nevertheless, creating a multiple year schedule that considers the constraint that every team must play at least once every few years is possible. If our team had more time, we would have created such a schedule. We also would have added two-week road trips so an away team could play two away games with roughly the same travel distance as one game. Although our current one-year schedule could not realistically be implemented, adding those two improvements might make a schedule that could actually compete with the NFL's current schedule.
