Background:

Company ABC is going to enter City XYZ selling a daily used, non-perishable product. Our team Analyzed this market entrance using two separate linear programming models to minimize Company ABC's costs.

Forecasting Demand:

Before we could create these models, we had to calculate the demand of each district of City XYZ. Company ABC recently completed a survey in each district regarding daily usage of similar products and percent of answers statistics for three categories: "Definitely Yes", "Yes", and "Maybe". Thomas Juster, a famous statistician, argued that customer survey questions typically contained underlying probability implications. Eric and Larry Risen, of BioTrak Research Inc., a market research firm, discussed a study in which Juster's scale was tested on individuals purchasing soup and yogurt. The surveyors then asked the customers which goods they actually purchased. Eric and Larry Risen used this data to interpret Juster's scale more accurately. Juster interpreted responses to "Certain, Practically Certain", "Very Probable", and "Fairly good possibility" as 99%, 80%, and 50% probability of purchase respectively. Eric and Larry Risen found that more accurate interpretations of these responses would be 83%, 66%, and 39% respectively. (Risen, 4) Company ABC's survey uses slightly different wording than Juster's surveys and they are selling neither yogurt nor soup. Nevertheless, Eric and Larry Risen's study involves customer follow up. Furthermore, the regression line that they used to create the response definitions had an R2 of .9435. (An R2 score that is close to 1 is an indication that their model is extremely accurate.) Therefore, I decided to interpret "Definitely Yes" as 83%, "Yes" as 69%, and "Maybe" as 39% purchase probability. (See appendix for exact demand calculations.) It is important to note that obtaining data based on customer feedback is not as accurate as time series forecasting methods. Once a few months of data has been generated, the supply chain manager should use data from actual store sales as opposed to the data generated from the customer survey to estimate average demand and standard deviation of demand.

Store Location Decisions:

Once our team calculated demand, our next step was to determine which stores to open and which districts to assign these stores to. We used a method called linear programming to make these decisions. Linear programming is a process in which decisions are made on variables given certain constraints. The goal of linear programming is to either minimize or maximize an equation called "the objective function". In our team's linear program, the goal was to minimize the cost of opening stores. The constraints in our linear program involved a sales minimum of 100 and a sales maximum of 226. (We wanted to leave a 24 unit cushion for possible fluctuations in demand.) Another important constraint was the maximum distance that a store could be from a district it served. If the maximum distance was low, then the cost of opening stores would be relatively small while shipping would be high. However, if the maximum distance was small, the cost of opening stores would be high while the cost of shipping would be low. Because we do not know where Company ABC's customers live, we do not know the cost of delivery. Nevertheless, since stores typically deliver to customers on a daily basis, we decided that constraining the maximum distance to a small number while incurring higher store costs was the best decision. Therefore, I constrained the maximum service distance for each district to ten miles except for district 9. (District 9's retail store is 14 miles away.) A maximum service distance of twenty miles incurred approximately $12,000 less in store costs. Nevertheless, "last mile delivery" is often one of the most expensive parts of a supply chain; the money saved from more efficient daily delivery routes would certainly outweigh the added store expense. The result of the 930 variable linear program was 17 retail stores assigned to 30 districts and a total cost of $69,400. (See Appendix)

Factory, Production, Inventory,and Delivery Decisions :

Once our group made decisions regarding retail stores and district assignments, we constructed a second linear program to make production, inventory, shipping, and production facility decisions. Since retail store to district relationships had been considered in the first model, this model only accounts for retail stores and production facilities. This linear program represents one week of operation in City XYZ for Company ABC. Inventory from day 7 is linked to day 1. Therefore, the program is a weekly plan that Company ABC could use over and over. (To start the weekly cycle, Company ABC would have to start with start with day 1 inventory levels as specified by the program.) If demand shifts dramatically, new demand numbers could be entered into the program and a new weekly plan could be developed within ten minutes. This new program would have the same production facilities because these facilities would have already been constructed. Nevertheless, production plans, shipping assignments, and inventory levels can always be adjusted in case demand fluctuates significantly.

The majority of the decision variables in the second linear program are either numeric variables for amounts shipped or binary variables representing trucks traveling from one point to another. These two sets of variables are linked so that when a certain amount of product is shipped from one point to another, a corresponding binary truck variable is activated. The "amount shipped" variables allow the program to make production, shipment, inventory decisions, while the "binary truck" variables allow the program to account for shipment costs between locations. Another feature of our model is that one truck is capable of making stops at multiple retail locations before returning to a factory. In fact, if a retail location records that a truck arrives and does not travel to another retail location, the truck is prompted to return to a factory. This feature has its own set of binary decision variables, signifying a return trip from any retail store to any existing factory. The last set of decision variables are facility decision variables which signify whether or not a production facility is constructed. These variables allow the program to consider shipment and inventory decisions at the same time as facility location decisions. The result of the 6,595 variable program is building facilities at locations 1, 2, and 3. A total of 29 trucks leave the factories and 93 total deliveries are made. Each retail store keeps between 8 to 11 units of safety stock at all times. The cost of one week's operation in the second linear program is approximately $220,000. Therefore, the total cost of one week of operation is approximately $289,000, not including daily van deliveries from retail stores to customer's homes. (See appendix for formulation and results.)

(3 important model 2 clarifications)

1)Trucks do not always return to their starting factory, but they always return to a factory that exists.

2) In some instances, A truck delivers 0 to a retail store. This only arises because the shortest distance between two points is not always a line in this data set. For example, from District 28 to District 2 (6 miles), District 2 to District 6 (5), District 6 to Factory 3(19) = District 28 to 3.(30) Thus, making multiple stops with 0 units is still optimal.

3) In two instances, "amounts shipped" figures not originating in the factories appear. However, the unaccounted units of product are not then sold. Furthermore, the model can still be interpreted as if they did not exist and they do not increase the objective function.

step2: deciding which retail stores to build and which districts they will serve

Decision Variables: 1) Whether or not a district contains a retail store (30 binary variables) 2) Whether or not each retail store serves each district (900 binary variables)

Objective Function: (only subset 1 is included in objective function)

SWhether or not a district contains a retail store * (fixed weekly operating cost + additional weekly store cost) = Minimize Costs

Constraints:

1) Each district is limited to one retail store

SPotential Retail assignments for each district = 1 (30 constraints, repeat for each district.)

2) Each district must be served by a retail store within a ten mile radius (except for district 9)

S(Potential Retail Assignment * Distance between stores) <= 10 (30 constraints, repeat for each district)

3) Retail Sales Maximum

S(district demand * whether or not a retail store is assigned to a district) <= 226 (30 constraints, repeat for each district)

4) Retail Sales Minimum

S(district demand * whether or not a retail store is assigned to a district) - 100 >= 100*(whether a district contains a store or not - 1) (30 constraints, repeat for each district)

(whether a district contains a store*30) >= Swhether or not a store serves a particular district

6) If a retail store is opened, it must serve its own district

whether or not a store exists >= whether or not that store serves itself

7) Non-negativity

Step 3: Production Facility, Shipment, Inventory, and Production Decisions

Decision Variables: Every day variables: Amount shipped from Factory to Stores, Amount Shipped from Stores to other Stores, Sales, Inventory, trucks from factory to stores(binary), trucks from stores to stores(binary), trucks from stores to factories(binary) Weekly Variables: facility decision (binary)

Objective Function: S(amount shipped from factory to store *5) + S(inventory*.05) + S(trucks from factory to stores * distance from factory to stores * 3) + S(trucks from stores to other stores * distance between stores * 3) + S(trucks from stores to factories * distance from factories to stores * 3) + S(facility decision * (fixed weekly operating cost + additional weekly facility cost))

Constraints:

1) Demand

Sales for every store = Demand for every store (119 constraints, repeat for every store every day)

2) inventory balance

Sales = (Amount shipped in - Amount shipped out) + (yesterday's inventory - today's inventory) (repeat for every store every day)

3) Safety Stock {(calculation at 95% significance: safety stock = 1.65*SQRT(avg lead time*standard deviation of demand)) Note: standard deviation of demand is estimated with (1-R2) * demand (R 2 originates from Risen's model)}

inventory >= safety stock (repeat for every store every day)

4) Factory to store amount shipped link

SAmount shipped from all factories to one store = Amount shipped from that store to itself (incurs shipping cost of 0) (Repeat for every store, every day)

5) Factory to store truck link

STrucks used from all factories to one store = Trucks used from that store to itself (Repeat every store every day)

6) Truck Return

SInward Trucks into a store = STrucks leaving store to go to another store + STrucks returning to a factory (Repeat every store every day)

7) Truck and Amount shipped link (links truck and amount shipped variables)

amount shipped <= (corresponding binary truck variable)* (repeat for every truck/amount shipped connection. truck return binary variables not included)

8) Amount received must be less than amount that leaves

amount shipped inward <= amount shipped outward (repeat for every store every day)

9) Whole Model Capacity

SEntire weekly output for one factory <= (Binary facility decision variable)*weekly factory capacity (repeat for every factory, 5 constraints)

10) Only factories ABC builds can have trucks return to them (another whole model constraint)

SEvery binary truck return variable for one factory <= 1000 * binary facility decision variable (repeat for every factory)

11)Non-negativity
